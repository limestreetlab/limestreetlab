Find $ \int e^{-4x} \cos(2x)\,dx$
Use integration by parts, namely $\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$.

Let $ u = e^{-4x} \implies \frac{du}{dx} = -4e^{-4x} \implies du = -4e^{-4x}\,dx $, and $ dv = \cos(2x)\,dx \implies v = \frac{1}{2}\sin(2x) $,
So, \begin{align} \int e^{-4x} \cos(2x)\,dx & = \frac{1}{2}e^{-4x}\sin(2x) - \int \frac{1}{2}\sin(2x)(-4e^{-4x}\,dx) \\ & = \frac{1}{2}e^{-4x}\sin(2x) + 2\int e^{-4x}\sin(2x)\,dx \end{align} For the second integral, let $ t = e^{-4x} \implies \frac{dt}{dx} = -4e^{-4x}\implies dt = -4e^{-4x}\,dx$, and $dz = \sin(2x)\,dx \implies z = -\frac{1}{2}\cos(2x)$,
so, $$ \int e^{-4x}\sin(2x)\,dx = -\frac{e^{-4x}}{2}\cos(2x) - 2\int e^{-4x}\cos(2x)\,dx $$ Note that the third integral is equal to the integral in question, so \begin{align} \int e^{-4x}\sin(2x)\,dx & = \frac{1}{2}e^{-4x}\sin(2x) + 2 \left( -\frac{e^{-4x}}{2}\cos(2x) - 2\int e^{-4x}\cos(2x)\,dx \right) \\ &= \frac{1}{2}e^{-4x}\sin(2x) - e^{-4x}\cos(2x) - 4\int e^{-4x}\cos(2x)\,dx \\ &= \frac{1}{5}\left( \frac{1}{2}e^{-4x}\sin(2x) - e^{-4x}\cos(2x) \right) \\ &= \frac{1}{10}e^{-4x}\sin(2x) - \frac{1}{5}e^{-4x}\cos(2x) \end{align}
