Suppose Sally has a fair coin and an unfair two-headed coin in her pocket.
She picks one at random and flips it; it lands head.
  1. What's the probability that the selected coin is the fair coin?
  2. She flips the same coin again and it lands head again. What's the (updated) probability that it is the fair coin?
  3. She flips the same coin again for the $3^\text{rd}$ time and it lands head again. What's the probability the coin is fair? What if instead it lands tail?
a.
Let $F$ and $B$ denote respectively the events that the coin is fair and biased (unfair). Also let $H_i$ denote that the $i^\text{th}$ flip is a head.
\begin{align} P(F | H_1) & = \frac{P(H_1 | F)P(F)}{P(H_1 | F)P(F)+P(H_1 | B)P(B)} \\ & = \frac{0.5 \cdot 0.5}{0.5 \cdot 0.5 + 1 \cdot 0.5} \\ & = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{4}{3} = \frac{1}{3} \end{align}
b.
Because coin flips are independent, we can update the conditional probability sequentially using the previous posterior probability as the prior probability. \begin{align} P(F | H_1 \cap H_2) & = \frac{P(H_2 | F)P(F | H_1)}{P(H_2 | F)P(F | H_1) + P(H_2 | B)P(B | H_1)} \\ & = \frac{0.5 \cdot 1/3}{0.5 \cdot 1/3 + 1 \cdot 2/3} \\ & = \frac{1}{6} \cdot \frac{6}{5} = \frac{1}{5} \end{align}
c.
Again, use the previous posterior probability as the prior probability. \begin{align} P(F | H_1 \cap H_2 \cap H_3) & = \frac{P(H_3 | F)P(F | H_1 \cap H_2)}{P(H_3 | F)P(F | H_1 \cap H_2) + P(H_3 | B)P(B | H_1 \cap H_2)} \\ & = \frac{0.5 \cdot 1/5}{0.5 \cdot 1/5 + 1 \cdot 4/5} \\ & = \frac{1}{10} \cdot \frac{10}{9} = \frac{1}{9} \end{align}
If it instead lands tail, it is obvious that the coin must be the fair one and the desired probability is $1$. \begin{align} P(F | H_1 \cap H_2 \cap H_3^c) & = \frac{P(H_3^c | F)P(F | H_1 \cap H_2)}{P(H_3^c | F)P(F | H_1 \cap H_2) + P(H_3^c | B)P(B | H_1 \cap H_2)} \\ & = \frac{0.5 \cdot 1/5}{0.5 \cdot 1/5 + 0 \cdot 4/5} \\ & = \frac{1/10}{1/10} = 1 \end{align}
