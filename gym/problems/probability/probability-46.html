Dealing from a standard 52-card deck, how many possible 5-card hands are there of:
  1. full-house (3 same values and 2 same values)
  2. straight flush (straight and flush), royal flush (straight flush of 10JQKA)
  3. flush
  4. straight
  5. one pair
  6. two pairs
  7. three of a kind
  8. four of a kind
  1. Full-house
    For the $3$ having the same value, there are $\binom{13}{1}$ for the value and $\binom{4}{3}$ for the suit. For the $2$ having the same value, there are $\binom{12}{1}$ for the value and $\binom{4}2{}$ for the suit. The number of possible full-house hands is therefore $$ \binom{13}{1} \cdot \binom{4}{3} \cdot \binom{12}{1} \cdot \binom{4}{2} = 13 \cdot 4 \cdot 12 \cdot 6 = 3744 $$
  2. Straight flush
    A straight can range from A2345 to 10JQKA; there are $10$ of these and there are $4$ suits. There are therefore $10 \cdot 4 = 40$ such hands, among which $4$ are royal flush.
  3. Flush
    Each suit consists of $13$ cards and there are $4$ suits; straight flush hands should be substracted as well. $$ \binom{13}{5} \cdot 4 - 40 = 5108 $$
  4. Straight
    There are 10 possible consecutive sequences and each card can have any of the $4$ suits; straight flush hands should be substracted as well. $$ 10 \cdot 4^5 - 40 = 10200 $$
  5. One pair
    The pair can be picked from any $13$ values and there are $_4 C_2$ ways for their suits. The remaining $3$ cards must take the remaining $12$ values and can be of any suits. There are $_12 C_3 \cdot 4^3$ ways for the remaining cards; alternatively, there are $48, 44, 40$ possible cards for the remaining cards but $48 \cdot 44 \cdot 40$ gives the ordered permutations so must be discounted by $3!$. $$ \binom{13}{1} \cdot \binom{4}{2} \cdot \binom{12}{3} \cdot 4^3 \text{or} \binom{13}{1} \cdot \binom{4}{2} \cdot 48 \cdot 44 \cdot 40 \cdot \frac{1}{3!} = 1098240 $$
  6. Two pairs
    There are $_13 C_2$ ways for the two pairs and for each pair there are $_4 C_2$ ways for the suits. There are then $(13 - 2) \cdot 4 $ remaining cards for the last card. $$ \binom{13}{2} \cdot {\binom{4}{2}}^2 \cdot 44 = 123552 $$
  7. Three of a kind
    There are $_13 C_1$ ways for the same value and $_4 C_3$ for the suits. There are $_12 C_2$ ways for the two values and each of them can have any suits. $$ \binom{13}{1} \cdot \binom{4}{3} \cdot \binom{12}{2} \cdot 4^2 = 54912 $$
  8. Four of a kind
    There are $_13 C_1$ ways for the same value and $_4 C_4$ for the suits. There are $_12 C_1$ ways for the remaining one value that can be of any suits. $$ \binom{13}{1} \cdot \binom{4}{4} \cdot \binom{12}{1} \cdot 4 = 624 $$
