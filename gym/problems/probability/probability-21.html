There are n $1$'s and m $0$'s. They are randomly permuted into a sequence.
So there are $(n+m)!/(n!m!)$ possible arrangements for the sequence, each equally likely.

Define a run of $1$'s as, $$ \text{a sequence of 1's, of any size including only a single 1 or many consecutive 1's} $$ For example, if $n=6, m=4$, the sequence $$ 1, 1, 1, 0, 1, 1, 0, 0, 1, 0\\ $$ has $3$ runs of $1$'s as below, $$ \underbrace{\boxed{1, 1, 1}, 0, \boxed{1, 1}, 0, 0, \boxed{1}, 0}_\text{there are $3$ stripes of $1$'s, so $3$ runs} $$
For any arbitrary n $1$'s and m $0$'s, compute the expected number of runs of $1$'s.
Also for runs of $0$'s and run's of both $0$'s and $1$'s.
According to the definition, a run of $1$'s is realized whenever an entry is $1$ but its prior entry is $0$.

Define an indicator, $$ I_i = \begin{cases} 1 \quad \text{ if a run starts at the $i^{th}$ position} \\ 0 \quad \text{ otherwise} \end{cases} $$ Let $R_1$ be the number of runs of $1$'s, which can be written as $$ R_1 = \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n+m} I_i $$ For $i$ = 1, because there is no prior entry, \begin{align} E(I_1) & = P(\text{a } 1 \text{ at } i =1) \\ & = \frac{n}{n+m} \end{align} For $1 \lt i \leq n+m,$ \begin{align} E(I_i) & = P(\text{$0$ at $i-1$ and $1$ at $i$}) \\ & = P(0 \text{ at } i-1) \cdot P(1 \text{ at } i) \\ & = \frac{m}{n+m} \frac{n}{n+m-1} \end{align} Hence, \begin{align} E(R_1) &= \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n+m} E(I_i) \\ & = \frac{n}{n+m} + (n+m-1)\left(\frac{nm}{(n+m)(n+m-1)}\right)\\ & = \frac{n}{n+m} + \frac{nm}{n+m} \\ & = \frac{n+nm}{n+m} \end{align} Similarly, $$ E(R_0) = \frac{m+nm}{n+m} $$ So, $$ E(R_{0 \& 1}) = \frac{n+nm}{n+m} + \frac{m+nm}{n+m} = 1 + \frac{2nm}{n+m} $$
