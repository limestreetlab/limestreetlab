  • Statistics Done Wrong
  • Alex Reinhart
  • This book is of type "lies, damn lies, and statistics", focusing on mis-interpretations of a few common tools, especially p-values.
    Author brings up the base rate fallacy and emphasizes that p-value measures the probability of type1 errors/false positives (coined in medical testing when the null is falsely rejected), namely P(significance | H0), and beta measures type2 errors/false-negatives, namely P(insignificance | H1). When we hear a test has a 1% p-value so is statistically significant at 5% alpha, we often mis-interpret that there is only a 1% chance of fluke so H1 gotta be the case. In fact that merely means P(significance | H0)=1%, no more no less, it doesn't at all touch upon H1; the thinking "so H1 gotta be the case" is a statement of P(H1 | significance)=very high, while the low p-value says P(significance | H0)=very low, which are highly related as p-value serves as the gatekeeper for significance (fluke blocker) but we also need to consider the base rate P(H1). The statistically significant elements include both true positives (through 1 - beta, ie "power of the test") and false positives (through alpha, ie cutoff), so in cases where base rate is very low, the error rate among significant elements = P(H0 | significance) = false positives / (true positives + false positives) is usually much higher than p-values P(significance | H0), rendering the test inherently unreliable and confidence level misleading. Hence, when we see a low p-value, we should note that it safeguards the test by setting a threshold on false positives but the likelihood of H1 given significance ("that gotta be the case") is also tied to the size of true positives, driven by the availability of positives (base rate) and power of the test in correctly detecting them.

    Other than that, he mostly complains about some methodological mistakes often seen in academic publications and what shall be done to mitigate them, which I have no interest whatsoever.
