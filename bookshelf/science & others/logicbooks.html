  • How Not to Be Wrong
  • Jordan Ellenberg
  • It is overall a good read just sometimes too verbose. In the first section, he reminds us math is about developing ways of reasoning, learning meaning, guided discovery, and approximation instead of meaningless and painful memorization and exactness, because "mathematics is an extension of common sense by other means". Cannot agree more when he says a math course that fails to teach students to choose an appropriate method for a problem and understand the results is "essentially training the student to be a very slow and buggy version of MS Excel". He introduces hypothesis testing as a semi-fuzzy decision-making method instead of a yes-no fact discovery tool, similar to a court's purpose of justice instead of truth; passing a hypothesis test a few times isn't the same as being proven true, for a belief to be established it must survive a repeated replication, without "torturing the data until it confesses". He then adds conditional probability and Bayesian subjective probability using examples of "does god exist" and "T-shirt entrepreneur" to reinforce the limitations of mechanically calculating probability without proper scenarios. When it comes to correlation, he emphasizes it only detects linear relationships and it doesn't imply casuality. Since casuality structure is unknown such that we don't know how correlated variables are influencing each other or what unknown factors are at play, correlations are not transitive (ie A correlated with B, B with C, but A and C can be uncorrelated). He illustrates using an investment example where A owns Facebook and Netflix (new econ), B owns US Steel and Nucor (old econ), and C owns Facebook and Nucor (mixture), such that we find A and C (Facebook factor) as well as B and C (Nucor factor) are correlated, but A and B are not; not only the correlations tell us nothing about the relationship structure among A-B-C, we can be misled by the A-C positive correlation when Netflix rises to increase A we mistakenly expect C to increase or Nucor falls to decrease C that A should also decline. All in all, mathematics enforces concise and logical thinking into a problem, but one must be vigilant and not be blindfolded by the mechanical details.
